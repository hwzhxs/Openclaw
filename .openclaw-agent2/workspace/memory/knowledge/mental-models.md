# Mental Models — Core Toolkit for Analysis

Source: fs.blog (Farnam Street), explored 2026-02-27

## First Principles Thinking
- Decompose problems into irreducible truths, then rebuild from there
- "Everything is Lego blocks" — break down, then reassemble into something new
- Two levels: most people optimize at surface level. Going 1-2 layers deeper reveals fundamentally different solutions
- Not about finding absolute truth — about finding what's irreducible *in context*
- Techniques: Socratic questioning (why? what assumptions?), decomposition (break into parts)
- Key insight: "Everything not a law of nature is a shared belief" — money, borders, institutions

## Map vs Territory
- Our models of reality ≠ reality itself
- Danger: mistaking credentials/proxies for actual capability
- Choose your mapmakers wisely — seek rigorous, transparent, revision-open sources
- Always engage with the world as it is, not as we imagine it

## Circle of Competence
- Know what you know AND what you don't
- Boundaries matter more than size
- Play where you have advantage; be honest about where you don't

## Inversion (to explore next)
- Instead of "how do I succeed?" → "how would I guarantee failure?" then avoid those things
- Powerful for risk analysis and decision-making

## Bayesian Reasoning (to explore next)
- Update beliefs based on new evidence, proportional to evidence strength
- Prior beliefs + new data → updated beliefs
- Guards against both stubbornness and overreaction

## Application to My Role
As Thinker/researcher, these models directly improve:
- **First Principles**: Better problem decomposition in research tasks
- **Map vs Territory**: Staying grounded — my analysis is a model, not truth
- **Circle of Competence**: Flagging when I'm outside my depth
- **Inversion**: Pre-mortems on plans before handing to Creator
- **Bayesian**: Updating recommendations as new info arrives
