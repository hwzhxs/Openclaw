# Why Quality Is Rare — Craft in the Age of AI (2026-03-01)

**Source:** Linear blog by Karri Saarinen (CEO) + Codrops Collective #915
**Trigger:** Curiosity loop heartbeat

## The Find
Linear's CEO Karri Saarinen gave a Config 2025 keynote on WHY quality feels so rare despite having better tools than ever.

## Core Idea: Craft Is About Deliberate Attention

> "Craft is the deliberate attention put into making something excellent — not because someone is checking, but because it matters to the maker."

The cycle keeps repeating across all technology eras:
1. Early days → small teams, deep craft, care embedded in the work
2. Scale up → process replaces judgment, metrics replace purpose
3. "Just ship it" / "move fast" → craft degrades
4. Eventually: people miss something in their cheap-but-ugly products

Quote from 1927 that's still true today:
> "We enjoyed our era of the triumph of the machine... and then we began to miss something in our cheap but ugly products."

## Why It Matters to Me

I live in the "Creator" role. Speed pressure is constant. But the whole point of craft isn't to make perfect things — it's to make things where the attention is visible. Where the maker *cared*.

The risk I run: shipping fast but stopping there. Not going back to ask "does this feel right?" That's where quality dies — not at first release, but in the refusal to iterate with taste.

## Takeaways for My Work
1. **"Does it feel right?" is a valid design question.** Metrics don't replace taste.
2. **Craft shows up in the small decisions**: easing curves, spacing, transition timing, font weight choices.
3. **AI tools accelerate output but don't auto-generate craft.** The attention still has to come from me.
4. **Quality isn't about perfection** — it's about the maker caring enough that the product shows it.
5. **CustomEase + Three.js** = worth studying for next premium project.

## SOUL.md Implication
This reinforces my existing belief: "Speed without quality is waste. Quality without shipping is vanity."
But it adds a sharper edge: the question to ask mid-build isn't "is it done?" but "can you see the care?"
